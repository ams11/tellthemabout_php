It's a short 60,000 miles around the world!


The all-over-the-globe Route

Stage 0, the Prelude
More Stages
Step 1 - fly to somewhere in India. Why India? It's not exactly anywhere near the rest of the route? Well... that's precisely why, actually! It may only be a subcontinent, but we can't just leave it out, plus this is where the Rickshaw Run starts! So,

Step 2 - on December 31, 2007, climb into a pair of 145cc, 7HP rickshaws and in roughly two weeks (let's not kid ourselves, it'll take us longer) pilot the said rickshaws from the yet-to-be-specified location at "the very southern tip of India" to Kathmandu, Nepal. Any further details are still a bit sketchy, but there's apparently some great snowboarding in January in the Himalayas!

Step 3 - There's the small matter of a six month break between the tentative end of the Rickshaw Run and the start of the Mongol Rally... The sensible thing to do here would probably be coming back home, and working for a few more months. However, none of this is about being sensible! Therefore, instead, spend these next six months traveling around Asia and Oceania... Some whirlwind combination of Thailand, Vietnam, Tibet, Singapore, Malaysia, and China should pass the time. This is also a good time for a detour to Australia and New Zealand, climb that continent's tallest peak in Mount Cook, New Zealand and drive some sort of a beat-up car all the way across the Australian outback.

Step 4 - back to the original plan! After aimlessly wondering for a few months, it's time to get back on track, sort of... So, we'll end up back in Kathmandu, head off to Tibet from there, visiting the Everest base camp along the way, and eventually ending up in Lhasa, the capital of ancient Tibet. From Lhasa, a train to Beijing, to Ulan Bator, Mongolia, up to Irkutsk, Russia, and via the trans-Siberian railway, to Moscow, where Stage 1 commences. 		 


Stage 1, the Mongol Rally
More Stages
This is, after all, about driving a wholly unworthy car all the way around the world, not riding around in rickshaws, earning frequent train rider miles, or hanging out with kangaroos in Australia! With the Mongol Rally about to start, it's time to acquire a car - the sensible choice is a Soviet-made Lada, a vehicle clearly designed for the atrocious roads that comprise most of the former Soviet Union (and ones prevalent, I'm sure, in South and Central America, Africa, far-eastern Asia, and anywhere else we'll end up). A far more entertaining choice is a Mini! Obviously not the almost safe and reliable version currently produced by BMW, rather the more classic version produced by Austin Metro! It would certainly be an intriguing debate to try and determine which car is better suited for the trip, but since there's four of us, we need two cars anyway... so, pick up a Lada in Russia, then pick up a Mini somewhere in Europe... preferably before reaching the British isles, or somebody is going to have to learn to deal with a steering wheel being on the wrong side! Both cars will have to be at least 25 years old, as otherwise we will not be able to bring them into the United States (sigh...).

Some time around the end of July, the 2008 Mongol Rally will launch from Hyde Park, London, and we'll be a part of it, along with the Lada and the Mini! From London, a downright leisurely jaunt across Western Europe to Prague, a somewhat more difficult drive across Eastern Europe into Istanbul, Turkey, and here the fun really starts as the route goes into the Ukraine, Russia, Kazakhstan, Uzbekistan and the rest of former Soviet Union/Central Asia eventually ending up in Ulan Bator, Mongolia some time at the end of August. Forming a precise route ahead of time for Central Asia is likely a waste of time, since it'll keep changing every step of the way, but hopefully we'll get to see Odessa, Volgograd, Samarqand, and Almaty, among many others along the way. For the Mongol Ralliers, the adventure is nearly over upon reaching Ulan Bator (just needing to find a way back), for us it's merely on to stage 2! 		 


Stage 2: the Road of Bones
More Stages
The next obvious question is how do we get the cars to the Pacific coast? There is once again a sensible option - simply load them onto a train in Mongolia, headed for Vladivostok. That's plainly far too sensible, so what should be the shortest stage of the trip potentially becomes the most dangerous portion, as the cars drive! Driving also offers a plethora of potential routes, but borrowing a page from the Long Way Round (who had a good enough idea to drive all the way around the world, but only got to see three of the seven continents along the way...), we will instead attempt to follow the Road of Bones from Yakutsk to Magadan, Russia! Admittedly, this is a terrible idea, and we may have to settle for simply crossing Siberia from Irkutsk to Vladivostok, but that would be downright disappointing... Upon reaching the Pacific coast, get the cars over to Japan, and after a bit of sight-seeing in Japan (climb Mt. Fuji...) load the cars onto a container ship headed for North America. Hopefully, we can catch a ride on the same ship... In North America, beg, borrow, and deal to get the cars into the US, which appears to have far and away the most draconian laws and regulations when it comes bringing cars into the country (I guess we're still a bit isolationist...). Of the assorted ways of solving the bureaucracy, taking cars at least 25 years old seems like the most straightforward...

*Disclaimer: at this point even I have to admit that driving the Road of Bones would be impractical at best, and likely impossible with the cars we're discussing in reality... So, I suppose it'll actually end up being some sort of a more sane route simply driving to Vladivostok... But I'd like to do this, and even if we don't drive it, taking a train/plane up to see both Yakutsk and Magadan (the two endpoints) might be worth the time and effort! 		 


Stage 3: the Pan-American highway
More Stages
The Pan American highway is a roughly 30,000 mile route (not precisely defined) from Fairbanks, Alaska to approximately Tierra del Fuego, at the very Southern tip of Argentina... this, naturally will be our next target. I can't even begin to anticipate the various details of the journey, but we'll need at least two detours along the way: first there's the Darien Gap - a little patch of jungle in southern Panama/northern Columbia, which is not only widely considered impassable (even by 'real' cars), but is also somewhat unstable, especially for visiting Americans... Second, there's Carnival - if we're going to be in South America during Carnival, we're going to have to detour to Rio de Janeiro, I don't care how out of the way it is! Along the way down south, visit Mt McKinley in Alaska, Mt. Aconcagua in Chile (the tallest peaks in North and South Americas, respectively), and Mt Chirripo in Costa Rica - the peak of which allows views of both the Pacific ocean and the Caribbean.  

Detour #1 - Antarctica
More Stages
Reaching the Southern tip of South America seems like an obvious time to take a brief trip to the 7th continent - Antarctica! As much fun as it would certainly be to drive all over it, and to visit its highest peak (the Vinson Massif), that seems rather unreasonable (even to me...), so simply walking on Antarctic soil (ice) will have to be enough...  
Stage 4: across the Atlantic!
More Stages
Stage 5 is the Dakar Rally route, so we will need to cross the Atlantic and bring the cars over to Senegal. Unfortunately, there's not a lot of information available about shipping from South America to Africa, so this will largely be an exploratory expedition. However, there are cargo shipping routes from several ports in Argentina and Brazil to Dakar, so upon returning from Antarctica, we will drive the cars North, along the Eastern seaboard of South America this time towards, likely, one of the Brazilian ports that'll ship the cars to Senegal for us...  
Stage 5: Dakar Rally and
back to London
More Stages
While it would be amusing to actually enter a pair of under-powered 25 year old cars into the Dakar Rally, nobody's going to let us (plus they run the rally the wrong way - ending, not starting, in Dakar). Instead, upon arriving in Dakar, we will drive the cars north along the African shore of the Atlantic Ocean into Morocco, crossing back over into Europe, via Lisbon to complete the Dakar rally route, and on back up to London to finish circumnavigating the globe! A few more sight-seeing stops along the way including Mt. Blanc (the tallest peak in Europe, if you're willing to discount Mt. Elbrus - is Georgia really part of Europe?). Africa also offers the enthralling possibility to cross the Sahara desert (we will have crossed the Gobi already in Mongolia, why not add Sahara to the conquered list?) - not immediately sure how feasible this would be, especially since our venerable cars wouldn't be suited for the trip, so we'd likely need to hire more appropriate transportation, but it's certainly a tempting possibility to consider - after all, if you're going to be visiting the Sahara desert, why not just drive across it already!?  


Detour #2 - Kilimanjaro
More Stages
It's probably a good time to take a quick break from the cars anyway... So far we will have visited the tallest peaks on each continent visited (except for Antarctica... and yes, you, Mt. Elbrus, in 'European' Georgia...), so it seems appropriate to take a detour and visit and climb beautifully scenic Mt Kilimanjaro, the tallest peak in Africa! As long as we're flying to take a bit of a detour, might as well visit a few more attractions in the area - the island of Crete, the pyramids of Egypt, ancient Israel, exotic Zanzibar, a safari in Tanzania, and opulent Dubai!  

Stage 6: What's the quickest
way from London to Miami?
More Stages
I'm not sure, but we're going to go through Iceland, which isn't exactly on the way, but this seems like the right time to visit it! So, drive the venerable cars north into Scotland and catch a ferry from the tip of the British Isles out to Iceland. After spending a bit of time in Iceland, it's back off to North America - unfortunately there aren't any ferries here, so it's back to freight shipping for the cars. We'll aim for delivering the cars somewhere in Canada, then primarily follow the East coast of the US and Canada as far south as we can go, passing Miami and ending up in Key West, Florida.  

Detour #3 - the Carribean
More Stages
I'll probably be sick and tired of driving by now, so why not stop and spend a little time lounging in the sun? What better way for a break than to park the cars in Southern Florida and spend a few weeks wondering around various islands in the Caribbean - I figure we should be able to catch a ride on sail boats to spend some time in the area, without completely breaking the bank.  
Stage 7: back home to Seattle
More Stages
Final stage - drive across the entirety of the US, from Miami, Florida to Seattle, WA. It's roughly a 3500 mile journey, if you take the most direct route. Since any sort of a direct route would really be against the spirit of the trip, we'll instead go directly West towards San Antonio, TX, down across the border into Mexico from there, and on to the Pacific coast in Baja California, and Tijuana. Back across the border into the US and San Diego, and back East into Arizona to see Phoenix and the Grand Canyon. Continue north from the Grand Canyon into Las Vegas and across the Nevada desert into Reno, and eventually Lake Tahoe. On into Oregon, visiting (and climbing) Mt Shasta and Mt Bachelor along the way. Upon arriving in Portland, OR we'll be a mere 3 hours from being back home in Seattle, so instead of making it simple, let's instead head back East, past Mt Hood into Idaho, and on into Yellowstone. After spending some time camping around Yellowstone, exit it North into Canada, up towards Calgary and across British Columbia back to Whistler, Vancouver, and Victoria. From Victoria, finally take a ferry back into the US and into Seattle! Mt Rainier is right here in Seattle, seems only appropriate to climb it also to finish the journey!
